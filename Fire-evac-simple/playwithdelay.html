Hope you have enjoyed playing with the fire variables! Let's introduce another key factor affecting evacuation process, which is the pre-movement time! What is pre-movement time? Pre-movement time is the amount of time it takes for one to evacuate from his or her room. You might be thinking: "What's the point of modelling that? Doesn't everyone leave their rooms immediately when a fire alarm sounds?" However, according to literature research as well as surveys conducted of students staying in Cinnamon College, people tend to procrastinate, grab their belongings as well as confirming the validity of the fire before actually evacuating. Thus, pre-movement delay does affect the total evacuation time, and thus our group found it neccessary for you to discover the effects of pre-movement delay on total evacuation time!


"Initial-delay-mean and Initial-delay-sd"

There are two controls that help control the delay time of all the minions in the model, which are the two sliders named "initial-delay-mean" and "initial-delay-sd". If you are familiar with statistics, the terms "mean" and "sd" should ring a bell. The delay for all minions is modelled after a normal distribution with mean equal to the value set by "initial-delay-mean" and standard deviation equal to the value set by "initial-delay-sd". If you increase the value of "initial-delay-mean", then the average delay of all minions will increase. If you increase the value of "initial-delay-sd", then the spread of delay times will be greater.

That is all you need to know for the controls! Do play around with the delay controls, and again adjust the control for the number-of-minions to see the significant effect of the change in delay time.

Note: In case you are wondering how does time in the model converts to time in real life, 1 unit of time in the model translates to 5 seconds in real life. Now you can play with realistic delay times! Have fun! :)
